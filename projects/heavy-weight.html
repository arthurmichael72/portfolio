Data Storytelling

Heavy Weight on Small Shoulders

Master's Thesis — Interactive Scrollytelling Data Story

Role

Designer, Researcher, Developer

Timeline

2024 – 2026

Tools

D3.js, GSAP ScrollTrigger, HTML/CSS/JS, Adobe Illustrator, Watercolor

Context

MS Thesis, Northeastern University

Tags

D3.js Scrollytelling Public Health Visual Rhetoric ANS Theory

Overview

An interactive, scroll-driven web experience communicating the impact of Seasonal Malaria Chemoprevention (SMC) in West Mamprusi Municipal, Ghana's North East Region. The project translates abstract health statistics into culturally meaningful visual narratives designed to be legible to low-literacy audiences.

The Problem

Every year, Ghana's health system collects thousands of data points on childhood malaria. Admissions, deaths, treatment coverage, intervention dosing records, etc. In West Mamprusi, a Municipal in Ghana's North East Region, Seasonal Malaria Chemoprevention (SMC) has been saving children's lives since 2020. The data proves it. But data, on its own, doesn't reach the people it describes.

A mother in Walewale doesn't understand rate ratios. A Community Health Volunteer moving from house to house to supervise the administration of the SMC drugs doesn't read confidence intervals. The numbers exist in a system designed for epidemiologists, not for the communities whose children are the numbers.

This isn't just a design problem. It's a communication challenge: communities must understand health information that affects them.

The Insight

Two bodies of research shaped my approach:

  • The Approximate Number System (ANS): Cognitive science tells us that humans lose the ability to intuitively distinguish large quantities. Our brains operate on ratios, not absolutes. "298,260 cases prevented" is meaningless to intuition. But "enough to fill Tamale Stadium five times over" that, a person can feel.
  • Visual Rhetoric: Every design choice is a rhetorical choice. Chart type, color, framing, and medium each encode an argument about what matters and how the audience should relate to the information. Standard epidemiological reporting makes a rhetorical choice too: it says this data is for experts. I wanted to make a different argument.

The question became: What if the medium itself could carry the message, not just the charts inside it?

The Approach

I translated abstract health statistics into culturally meaningful comparisons using the Approximate Number System, grounded in the agricultural and community context of West Mamprusi.

I integrated three strategies:

  • Anchor: Ground large numbers in physically familiar referents. Not "298,260 cases" but Tamale Stadium, painted in watercolor so the reader sees scale, not digits.
  • Chunk: Break aggregates into human-scale units. Not "87.6 deaths per month" but "one child every 8.33 hours", a rate that fits within the rhythm of a day.
  • Narrate: Structure the data inside a story the audience can follow emotionally. I adopted the WHO Storytelling Handbook arc: Setup, Rising Tension, Climax, and Resolution, designed specifically for health communication impact.

The Data

Before designing anything, I needed to know what the data actually said. I analyzed West Mamprusi health system records from 2015 to 2024, five years before SMC and five years after.

What I found:

  • Admission reduction: 32.7% decline in childhood malaria admissions
  • Overall mortality reduction: 54.3% fewer childhood malaria deaths
  • Peak-season mortality reduction: 89.1% during active SMC months (July–October)
  • Cases prevented: 298,260 over five years
  • Deaths averted: 2,856 over five years

Process

Phase 1 — Visual Language

A series of watercolor illustrations establishing the world of the piece: a woman looking at the clouds with ambivalence, an empty playground, a community health worker on rounds, Tamale Stadium, and children on a playground. Each illustration serves a narrative function, not decoration but rhetoric. The opening woman establishes the burden. The stadium anchors magnitude. The closing caregivers embodies resolution.

Phase 2 — Data Integration

The idea was integrating data visualizations with watercolor illustration without aesthetic collision. I developed three visualization types:

  • Icon arrays: Grids of human figures showing malaria spike during the rainy season. The shift from red-dominant to green-dominant arrays is the visual summary of the entire story, visually understood before you read a single number.
  • ISOTYPE-inspired bar charts: Year-over-year admission and mortality trends in a restrained palette that breathes alongside the watercolors rather than fighting them.
  • Stadium anchor: A watercolor rendering of Tamale Stadium with its capacity noted, translating case counts into a physical space the audience can picture themselves inside.

Phase 3 — Narrative Architecture

I structured the piece using the WHO Storytelling Handbook story arc:

  • Setup: Title scene. The woman looking at the clouds. Opening text establishes West Mamprusi, childhood malaria, the stakes. Emotional register: empathy and curiosity. Cognitive load: low.
  • Rising Tension: Baseline burden data. Icon arrays fill with red. The stadium appears — "this many children." Bar charts show the relentless seasonal pattern. Each scroll reveals more weight. Emotional register: urgency. Cognitive load: building.
  • Climax: SMC arrives. The bar charts shift. The icon arrays turn green. The numbers prove it: 89.1% peak-season mortality reduction. This is the moment where accumulated tension meets evidence of transformation. Emotional register: relief. Cognitive load: peak, then release.
  • Resolution: Children playing. Grandmothers harvesting groundnuts. Summary statistics are restated, but the reader has already understood. The data has become a story about the continuation of families, of communities, of life. Emotional register: warmth. Cognitive load: settling.

Phase 4 — Web Implementation

The static composite was converted into an interactive scrollytelling webpage using Vibe coding with Google AI Studio—AI-assisted code generation producing HTML, CSS, and JavaScript for scroll-driven interactivity. This enabled rapid prototyping without extensive front-end development, translating the designed pacing and progressive disclosure into a functional web experience. The interactive version is hosted on GitHub, with plans for dedicated domain hosting to support broader access.
